# DGCA Civil Aviation Requirements — Flight Operations

## Document Reference
- **Authority**: Directorate General of Civil Aviation, India (DGCA)
- **Applicable To**: All Indian scheduled and non-scheduled air transport operators
- **Revision**: Rev 12, dated 15 January 2026
- **Effective Date**: 01 March 2026

---

## CAR Section 7 — Flight Operations

### Series J, Part I: Flight Duty Time Limitations (FDTLs)

#### 7.J.1.1 Purpose and Applicability
These regulations prescribe Flight Duty Time Limitations (FDTLs) and rest requirements for all flight crew members and cabin crew employed by Indian operators. The objective is to ensure that crew fatigue does not compromise the safety of flight operations. All operators holding an Air Operator's Permit (AOP) issued under Rule 134 of the Aircraft Rules, 1937 shall comply with these limitations.

#### 7.J.1.2 Definitions
- **Flight Duty Period (FDP)**: The total time from reporting for duty (which includes pre-flight preparation) to the time the engines are shut down at the end of the final sector. Reporting time shall be no less than 60 minutes before scheduled departure for domestic operations and 90 minutes for international operations.
- **Flight Time**: The total time from the moment an aircraft first moves under its own power for the purpose of taking off until the moment it finally comes to rest at the end of the flight. Also referred to as block-to-block time or chock-to-chock time.
- **Rest Period**: A continuous and uninterrupted period of time during which a crew member is free from all duties and is not on standby. Rest begins when the crew member reaches suitable accommodation.
- **Split Duty**: A Flight Duty Period that contains a break on the ground of defined minimum duration at a point other than the home base, during which the crew member is provided with suitable rest accommodation.
- **Augmented Crew**: A flight crew that comprises more than the minimum number of crew members required to operate the aircraft, allowing in-flight relief during cruise phase.
- **Window of Circadian Low (WOCL)**: The period between 0200 and 0559 hours local time at the crew member's acclimatized time zone, representing the period of maximum physiological sleepiness.

#### 7.J.1.3 Maximum Flight Duty Period Limits

##### 7.J.1.3.1 Two-Crew Operations (Standard)
| Start Time (Local) | 1 Sector | 2 Sectors | 3 Sectors | 4+ Sectors |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0600–0659 | 12:00 | 11:30 | 11:00 | 10:30 |
| 0700–1159 | 12:00 | 11:30 | 11:00 | 10:30 |
| 1200–1359 | 12:00 | 11:30 | 11:00 | 10:00 |
| 1400–1759 | 11:00 | 10:30 | 10:00 | 09:30 |
| 1800–2159 | 11:00 | 10:30 | 10:00 | 09:00 |
| 2200–0559 | 10:00 | 09:30 | 09:00 | 08:30 |

The maximum FDP for any two-crew operation shall not exceed **12 hours** under the most favorable conditions (early morning start, single sector). Operators shall reduce these limits by 30 minutes for each additional sector beyond the first.

##### 7.J.1.3.2 Augmented Crew Operations (3 or 4 Pilots)
- **Three-pilot crew**: Maximum FDP of 15 hours, provided adequate bunk rest facilities are available on board.
- **Four-pilot crew (double crew)**: Maximum FDP of 18 hours, provided class 1 rest facilities (horizontal sleeping berths isolated from the cockpit and cabin) are available.

##### 7.J.1.3.3 Extensions
The operator may extend FDP by up to 2 hours in unforeseen operational circumstances (e.g., ATC delays, weather diversions) provided:
1. The commander considers the crew fit for the extension.
2. The total extension does not cause the FDP to exceed 14 hours for two-crew operations.
3. All extensions are reported to the operator's flight safety department within 24 hours.
4. The subsequent rest period is increased by the duration of the extension.

#### 7.J.1.4 Maximum Flight Time Limits

| Period | Maximum Flight Time |
|---|---|
| Single duty period | 8 hours (may be extended to 10 hours with augmented crew) |
| Any consecutive 7-day period | 30 hours |
| Any consecutive 14-day period | 55 hours |
| Any consecutive 28-day period | 100 hours |
| Any consecutive 90-day period | 270 hours |
| Any calendar year | 1,000 hours |

These are absolute limits that cannot be exceeded regardless of crew augmentation or any other factor. Operators must maintain systems that track cumulative flight time and proactively prevent scheduling that would breach these limits.

#### 7.J.1.5 Minimum Rest Requirements

##### 7.J.1.5.1 Minimum Rest Between Duty Periods
The minimum rest period before the start of an FDP shall be at least:
- **12 hours** or the length of the preceding duty period, whichever is greater.
- This rest must include the opportunity for at least **8 hours of uninterrupted sleep** in suitable accommodation.
- Travel time to and from accommodation shall not count as rest.

##### 7.J.1.5.2 Weekly Rest
- Every crew member shall receive a minimum of **36 consecutive hours** free from all duties in any 7-day period.
- This 36-hour weekly rest must include two consecutive local nights (defined as the period between 2200 and 0800 local time).

##### 7.J.1.5.3 Extended Rest After Long-Haul Operations
- After any FDP exceeding 13 hours, the subsequent rest period shall be at least **18 hours**.
- After crossing more than 4 time zones, an additional recovery period of **24 hours** shall be provided before the next duty assignment.

#### 7.J.1.6 Fatigue Risk Management

##### 7.J.1.6.1 Fatigue Risk Management System (FRMS) Requirements
All operators with a fleet of 10 or more aircraft shall implement a Fatigue Risk Management System (FRMS) that includes:

1. **Fatigue Risk Scoring**: A scientifically validated model (such as SAFTE-FAST, Bio-mathematical models, or equivalent DGCA-approved models) shall be used to assess fatigue risk for each planned flight schedule. Schedules with a fatigue risk score exceeding the operator's defined threshold shall be redesigned or mitigated.

2. **Fatigue Reporting System**: A confidential, non-punitive reporting mechanism through which crew members can report fatigue events, fatigue-related incidents, and suggest improvements to scheduling practices.

3. **Fatigue Education and Training**: All flight crew, cabin crew, and rostering personnel shall receive initial and recurrent training on fatigue causes, symptoms, countermeasures, and reporting obligations.

4. **Monitoring and Review**: The FRMS shall include ongoing monitoring of crew fatigue indicators, including:
   - Analysis of flight data monitoring (FDM) parameters that may indicate fatigue (e.g., deviations during approach, extended reaction times)
   - Review of voluntary fatigue reports
   - Trend analysis of fatigue-related events quarterly

##### 7.J.1.6.2 Fatigue Risk Score Calculation
Operators shall assign a fatigue risk score on a scale of 1 to 10 for each planned duty:
- **Score 1–3 (Low Risk)**: No additional mitigation required.
- **Score 4–6 (Moderate Risk)**: Crew briefing on fatigue countermeasures required; controlled rest in cruise may be planned.
- **Score 7–8 (High Risk)**: Schedule redesign preferred. If not possible, additional crew member required and post-duty rest extended by 4 hours.
- **Score 9–10 (Unacceptable)**: Schedule shall not be operated. Immediate redesign mandatory.

Factors influencing fatigue score include: time since last sleep opportunity, duty start time relative to WOCL, number of sectors, cumulative duty in the preceding 7 days, time zone crossings, and whether the duty extends into the WOCL.

#### 7.J.1.7 Standby and Reserve Duty
- **Airport Standby**: Maximum 6 hours. If a crew member is assigned a flight during standby, the FDP starts at standby report time. Suitable rest facilities must be provided at the airport.
- **Home Standby**: Maximum 16 hours. FDP starts when the crew member is required to report. Response time shall not be less than 90 minutes from notification.
- If no duty is assigned during the standby period, it shall be followed by a minimum rest period of 10 hours.

### Series J, Part II: Pre-Flight Dispatch Requirements

#### 7.J.2.1 Dispatch Responsibility
The dispatch of every flight is a shared responsibility between the Pilot in Command (PIC) and the licensed Flight Dispatcher. Neither party may dispatch a flight unless both are satisfied that all regulatory and operational requirements have been met.

#### 7.J.2.2 Pre-Flight Dispatch Checklist
Before dispatching any scheduled flight, the operator shall verify the following minimum items:

**Aircraft Readiness:**
1. Valid Certificate of Airworthiness (C of A) — must be current and not expired.
2. Valid Certificate of Registration.
3. All applicable Airworthiness Directives (ADs) complied with — no overdue mandatory inspections.
4. Minimum Equipment List (MEL) items reviewed — all deferred items within permissible deferral period.
5. Aircraft technical log reviewed for open defects.
6. Fuel quantity verified against flight plan requirements (trip fuel + contingency + alternate fuel + final reserve + extra fuel as required).
7. Weight and balance computed and within limits.
8. Emergency equipment serviceable (ELT, life vests, fire extinguishers, oxygen systems).

**Crew Readiness:**
9. PIC and all flight crew hold valid licenses and ratings for the aircraft type and route.
10. Medical certificates current and valid.
11. Crew duty time within FDTL limits — no crew member approaching or exceeding any limit.
12. Crew rest requirements met — minimum 12 hours rest before duty.
13. Route and aerodrome qualification current — recency of 90-day experience for the route/aerodrome.
14. Crew composition meets regulatory requirements (minimum crew plus any augmentation needed).
15. Fatigue risk score within acceptable limits.

**Weather and Route:**
16. Departure weather meets or exceeds published minima.
17. Destination weather meets or exceeds the applicable approach minima.
18. Alternate airport weather meets or exceeds alternate minima.
19. En-route weather reviewed — no hazardous conditions (CB, volcanic ash, severe turbulence, icing) that cannot be safely navigated.
20. NOTAMs reviewed for departure, en-route, destination, and alternate aerodromes.
21. Significant weather charts (SIGWX) reviewed.

**Operational:**
22. ATC flight plan filed.
23. Operational flight plan prepared and signed by PIC and dispatcher.
24. NOTAM package complete and current.
25. Navigation databases current.
26. Performance calculations (takeoff, landing) completed for actual conditions.

#### 7.J.2.3 Go/No-Go Decision Authority
The final authority for the Go/No-Go decision rests jointly with the PIC and the dispatcher. Either party may independently cancel or delay the flight if they determine any safety concern exists. Disagreements shall be escalated to the Chief Operations Officer, but in no case shall a flight be dispatched over the objection of either the PIC or the dispatcher.

#### 7.J.2.4 Documentation Requirements
A complete dispatch record shall be maintained for each flight, including:
- Date and flight number
- Aircraft registration and type
- Crew names and license numbers
- Fuel calculations
- Weather briefing summaries
- NOTAM review acknowledgment
- MEL deferred items list
- Go/No-Go decision with signatures (electronic signatures acceptable)

These records shall be retained for a minimum of 90 days and shall be available for inspection by DGCA at any time.

## CAR Section 8 — Airworthiness

### Series S, Part I: Airworthiness Standards and Certification

#### 8.S.1.1 Certificate of Airworthiness (C of A)
Every aircraft registered in India shall hold a valid Certificate of Airworthiness issued by DGCA. The C of A is renewable annually, subject to:
1. Satisfactory completion of annual airworthiness review by a DGCA-approved organization.
2. Compliance with all applicable Airworthiness Directives.
3. Maintenance performed in accordance with the approved maintenance program.
4. Aircraft conforming to its approved type design or having approved supplemental type certificates (STCs) for any modifications.

#### 8.S.1.2 C of A Renewal Process
1. The operator shall apply for renewal at least 30 days before the expiry date.
2. A DGCA-approved airworthiness review staff (ARS) member shall conduct a physical inspection of the aircraft.
3. All maintenance records shall be reviewed to verify compliance with the approved maintenance program.
4. A flight test may be required at the discretion of DGCA.
5. The renewed C of A is valid for a period of one year from the date of issue.

An aircraft with an expired C of A shall not be operated for any purpose other than repositioning to a maintenance facility, and only with specific DGCA approval.

### Series S, Part II: Minimum Equipment List (MEL)

#### 8.S.2.1 MEL Overview
The Minimum Equipment List (MEL) is a document approved by DGCA that permits an operator to dispatch an aircraft with certain items of equipment inoperative, provided specific conditions and limitations are observed. The MEL is derived from the Master Minimum Equipment List (MMEL) issued by the aircraft type certificate holder and approved by the State of Design.

#### 8.S.2.2 MEL Deferral Categories and Time Limits

| Category | Repair Interval | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | As specified (typically 1–3 calendar days) | Items in this category shall be repaired within the time interval specified. The time begins at the moment the defect is discovered or deferred. Typical interval: **3 calendar days** (72 hours), not including the day the defect was discovered. |
| **B** | 3 consecutive calendar days + day of discovery, typically **10 calendar days** total | Items that may be deferred for a longer period. The operator must track these items and plan maintenance within the 10-day window. Extensions require DGCA approval. |
| **C** | 10 consecutive calendar days + day of discovery, typically **120 calendar days** total | Items that do not significantly affect airworthiness or operational capability. Must be repaired within 120 calendar days. The operator shall document the deferral in the aircraft technical log and track it through their maintenance tracking system. |
| **D** | No specific limit (as per MMEL) | Items with no time limit for repair, but these must still be tracked and repaired at the next available opportunity. These are typically items where redundancy is built into the system design. Operators shall review Category D deferrals at each C-check or every 18 months, whichever comes first. |

#### 8.S.2.3 MEL Deferral Conditions
When dispatching with an MEL item deferred:
1. The (O) maintenance procedure (if any) shall be accomplished before dispatch.
2. The (M) operations procedure (if any) shall be observed during flight operations.
3. Placards shall be placed as required.
4. The PIC shall be briefed on all deferred MEL items and shall acknowledge the briefing in the dispatch documentation.
5. Multiple MEL deferrals shall be reviewed collectively — the combined effect of multiple deferred items on aircraft systems, redundancy, and safety shall be assessed. The dispatch shall not proceed if the cumulative effect compromises safety.

#### 8.S.2.4 MEL Deferral Limits
- No more than **10 concurrent Category A or B deferrals** on a single aircraft.
- No more than **25 total concurrent deferrals** across all categories on a single aircraft.
- If deferral limits are reached, the aircraft shall be grounded for maintenance until deferrals are cleared to within limits.
- ETOPS operations: Specific MEL restrictions apply. Items critical to ETOPS (e.g., cargo fire suppression, engine-driven generators, APU) cannot be deferred for ETOPS flights.

### Series S, Part III: Airworthiness Directives (ADs)

#### 8.S.3.1 Compliance with Airworthiness Directives
Airworthiness Directives issued by DGCA, EASA, FAA, or the State of Design for the aircraft type shall be complied with within the specified timeframe. Non-compliance with an AD renders the aircraft unairworthy.

#### 8.S.3.2 AD Categories
- **Emergency AD**: Must be complied with before the next flight. The aircraft shall not be dispatched until the AD is accomplished.
- **Priority AD**: Must be complied with within the number of flight hours or calendar days specified, typically within 10 days or 50 flight hours.
- **Standard AD**: Must be complied with within the specified interval, which may range from 30 days to the next scheduled maintenance check.
- **Repetitive AD**: Requires recurring inspections or actions at specified intervals.

## CAR Section 7 — Crew Qualification Requirements

### 7.Q.1.1 Pilot Qualifications for Domestic Operations
For domestic operations within Indian airspace, flight crew shall meet the following minimum requirements:
1. Valid Airline Transport Pilot License (ATPL) for PIC, or Commercial Pilot License (CPL) with instrument rating for First Officer.
2. Current type rating for the specific aircraft type.
3. Minimum recency: 3 takeoffs and landings in the preceding 90 days on the same aircraft type.
4. Valid Class 1 Medical Certificate (renewed every 6 months for pilots over 40, annually for pilots under 40).
5. Completion of line check within the preceding 12 months.
6. Completion of proficiency check (base check) within the preceding 6 months.
7. Emergency and safety equipment training current (within 12 months).
8. CRM (Crew Resource Management) training completed within the preceding 24 months.
9. Dangerous Goods Regulations (DGR) training current (within 24 months).

### 7.Q.1.2 Additional Requirements for International Operations
In addition to domestic requirements, crew operating international routes shall:
1. Hold a valid ICAO English Language Proficiency endorsement at Level 4 (Operational) or above.
2. Have completed international route familiarization for the specific route or region.
3. Be briefed on the specific regulatory requirements of the destination state.
4. For ETOPS operations: Hold ETOPS-specific qualification with recurrent training within the preceding 12 months.
5. For operations to airports requiring special qualification (e.g., Leh, Paro, Lukla): Hold specific airport qualification with recency within the preceding 90 days.

### 7.Q.1.3 Cabin Crew Qualification Requirements
1. Valid cabin crew competency certificate issued by DGCA.
2. Aircraft type-specific training completed (initial or transition).
3. Annual recurrent training completed, including emergency drills, first aid, and firefighting.
4. Security training current (within 12 months).
5. Minimum crew complement: As specified in the operations manual for the aircraft type, but never less than 1 cabin crew per 50 passenger seats.

## CAR Section 7 — Route and Aerodrome Requirements

### 7.R.1.1 Aerodrome Operating Minima
Operators shall establish aerodrome operating minima for each aerodrome used, taking into account:
1. The type of approach procedure (ILS CAT I/II/III, VOR, RNAV, visual).
2. Aircraft performance and equipment.
3. Crew qualification and experience.
4. Aerodrome physical characteristics (runway length, width, slope, obstacles).

### 7.R.1.2 Weather Minima for Dispatch
- **Departure**: Visibility must meet or exceed the published takeoff minima. If visibility is below minima, the flight shall not be dispatched.
- **Destination**: The weather at the estimated time of arrival (ETA) must be forecast to be at or above the applicable approach minima. If the forecast indicates weather below minima, an alternate airport must be designated.
- **Alternate Airport**: Required if the destination weather forecast indicates any possibility of conditions below minima. The alternate must have weather forecast at or above alternate minima at the ETA.

### 7.R.1.3 NOTAM Review Requirements
Before every dispatch, the following NOTAMs shall be reviewed:
1. Departure aerodrome NOTAMs (runway closures, navigation aid outages, taxiway restrictions).
2. En-route NOTAMs (airspace restrictions, temporary danger areas, navigation aid outages).
3. Destination aerodrome NOTAMs.
4. Alternate aerodrome NOTAMs.
5. FIR NOTAMs for all Flight Information Regions along the route.

The NOTAM review shall be documented and acknowledged by both the PIC and the dispatcher.
