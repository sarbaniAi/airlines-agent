## SOP-001: Pre-Flight Readiness Checklist Procedure

### 1.1 Purpose
This procedure defines the comprehensive pre-flight readiness verification process that must be completed before any Air India flight is dispatched. The checklist covers approximately 250 individual verification items grouped into functional categories. This SOP ensures compliance with DGCA CAR Section 7 Series J Part II and the operator's Operations Manual Part A.

### 1.2 Scope
Applicable to all scheduled and charter flights operated by Air India and its subsidiary carriers. Every flight dispatch requires completion of this procedure by the assigned Flight Dispatcher in coordination with the Pilot in Command (PIC).

### 1.4 Checklist Completion Time
The full pre-flight checklist process typically requires **45–75 minutes** for domestic flights and **60–90 minutes** for international flights, starting from the time the flight dispatcher begins the dispatch preparation (typically 2 hours before scheduled departure).

## SOP-002: Go/No-Go Decision Criteria and Escalation Matrix

### 2.1 Go/No-Go Decision Framework
The Go/No-Go decision is the final safety gate before a flight is dispatched. This decision is made jointly by the Flight Dispatcher and the PIC, with escalation paths for disagreements or marginal situations.

### 2.2 Automatic No-Go Conditions
The following conditions result in an **automatic No-Go** — no exceptions, no escalation override:

1. **Certificate of Airworthiness expired or invalid.**
2. **Overdue Airworthiness Directive (any category).**
3. **Crew duty time will be exceeded before estimated block-in time.**
4. **Crew rest requirement not met (less than 12 hours prior rest).**
5. **PIC or First Officer license/type rating/medical certificate expired.**
6. **Fuel on board less than minimum required fuel.**
7. **Departure weather below published takeoff minima.**
8. **Destination and alternate weather both below applicable approach minima with no improvement forecast.**
9. **Aircraft structural damage not cleared by engineering (beyond MEL/CDL scope).**
10. **CVR or FDR inoperative (for aircraft >19 pax seats — per AD-2026-GEN-007).**
11. **TCAS/ACAS inoperative.**
12. **EGPWS/TAWS inoperative.**
13. **Fatigue risk score 9 or 10 for any crew member.**
14. **Security threat not cleared by security authority.**

### 2.3 Conditional Go Criteria (Requires Review)
The following conditions require documented review and risk assessment before a Go decision:

| Condition | Required Action | Authority to Approve |
|---|---|---|
| Single MEL Category A deferral | Review impact, document in dispatch release | Dispatcher + PIC |
| Multiple MEL deferrals (3+) | Engineering review of combined effect | Dispatcher + PIC + Duty Engineer |
| Destination weather marginal (at or near minima) | Confirm alternate available with adequate weather; confirm crew qualified for low-vis ops | Dispatcher + PIC |
| Crew fatigue risk score 7–8 | Add mitigation (controlled rest plan, additional crew if available) | Dispatcher + PIC + Crew Scheduling Manager |
| Delay exceeding 2 hours | Re-evaluate crew duty time, fuel, weather, and slot availability | Dispatcher + PIC |
| Volcanic ash advisory within 200nm of route | Re-route or delay; consult Met department | Dispatcher + PIC + Chief Dispatcher |
| Crosswind component within 5 knots of limit | Confirm PIC recency and experience; consider alternate runway | Dispatcher + PIC |
| Runway contamination (wet, snow, ice) | Confirm landing distance adequate with contamination correction; confirm braking action acceptable | Dispatcher + PIC |
| Single engine inoperative (for ferry flight) | Separate authorization per Operations Manual Part D | VP Flight Ops + Chief Engineer |

### 2.4 Escalation Matrix

| Level | Escalated To | When to Escalate | Response Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | Senior Dispatcher on Duty | Dispatcher and PIC disagree on a Conditional Go item | 10 minutes |
| Level 2 | Chief Dispatcher | Level 1 cannot resolve; or multiple Conditional Go items present simultaneously | 15 minutes |
| Level 3 | Duty Manager — IOCC | Significant operational impact (cancellation, diversion, aircraft swap); or safety concern raised by any party | 15 minutes |
| Level 4 | VP Flight Operations | Safety matter cannot be resolved at Level 3; or regulatory interpretation required | 30 minutes |
| Level 5 | Accountable Manager (Airline CEO) | Safety matter of highest severity; potential fleet-wide impact | As required |

### 2.5 Documentation
All Go/No-Go decisions shall be documented in the dispatch system with:
- Decision (Go / No-Go / Delayed)
- Timestamp
- Reason (if No-Go or Delayed)
- Persons involved in the decision
- Any escalation history
- Conditions accepted and mitigations applied (for Conditional Go)

## SOP-003: Crew Swap Procedures During Dispatch

### 3.1 When Crew Swap Is Required
A crew swap during the dispatch process may be necessary when:
- A crew member reports unfit for duty (illness, fatigue).
- Crew duty time will be exceeded due to a delay.
- License, medical, or qualification issue discovered during pre-flight checks.
- Security clearance issue identified.
- Crew member fails alcohol/drug test.

### 3.2 Crew Swap Process
1. **Identification**: The dispatcher identifies the crew member to be replaced and the reason.
2. **Notification**: Immediately notify Crew Scheduling/Crew Control.
3. **Replacement Sourcing**: Crew Control identifies a replacement from:
   - Airport standby crew (first priority — fastest response).
   - Home standby crew (second priority — 90-minute response time).
   - Crew from a cancelled or delayed flight (third priority).
   - Deadheading crew (fourth priority — least preferred due to duty time implications).
4. **Qualification Check**: Before confirming the replacement, verify:
   - Valid license and type rating for the aircraft.
   - Current medical certificate.
   - Route/airport qualification (if applicable).
   - Duty time and rest compliance — the replacement crew member must be within FDTL limits for the entire planned duty.
   - Recency requirements met.
5. **Briefing**: The replacement crew member shall receive a full dispatch briefing including:
   - All MEL deferred items.
   - Weather briefing.
   - NOTAM summary.
   - Any operational considerations specific to the flight.
6. **Documentation Update**: Update the dispatch release, load sheet (crew weight), and notification to ATC (if required).
7. **Delay Assessment**: If the crew swap causes a delay:
   - Re-assess slot time availability.
   - Re-check destination weather for new ETA.
   - Re-verify fuel adequacy.
   - Communicate revised ETD to all stakeholders.

### 3.3 Maximum Acceptable Delay for Crew Swap
- **Domestic flights**: Up to 90 minutes delay for crew swap before considering cancellation.
- **International flights**: Up to 120 minutes delay before considering cancellation.
- Beyond these thresholds, escalate to Level 3 (Duty Manager — IOCC) for cancellation vs. further delay decision.

## SOP-004: Weather Minimum Requirements

### 4.1 Departure Weather Minima
- **Visibility**: Must meet or exceed the published takeoff minimum for the runway in use.
  - Standard: RVR 550 meters (or equivalent visibility) for two-engine aircraft.
  - Low Visibility Takeoff (LVTO): RVR 200 meters minimum, if approved and crew qualified.
- **Ceiling**: No minimum ceiling required for takeoff, but if ceiling is below 200 feet AGL, Low Visibility Procedures (LVP) must be in effect.
- **Wind**: Crosswind within aircraft-type and crew-experience limits. Tailwind within aircraft limit for the runway.
- **Contamination**: If runway is contaminated (water, snow, ice, slush), takeoff performance must be recalculated using contaminated runway data.

### 4.2 Destination Weather Minima
- **ILS CAT I**: Decision Height 200 feet AGL, RVR 550 meters.
- **ILS CAT II**: Decision Height 100 feet AGL, RVR 300 meters (crew and aircraft must be CAT II approved).
- **ILS CAT IIIA**: Decision Height 50 feet AGL (or no DH), RVR 200 meters.
- **ILS CAT IIIB**: No Decision Height, RVR 75 meters (full autoland required).
- **VOR/NDB approach**: Minimum Descent Altitude as published, visibility as published (typically 1,500–3,000 meters).
- **RNAV (GNSS) approach**: As published, typically equivalent to or slightly higher than ILS CAT I.
- **Circling approach**: As published, typically ceiling 500 feet AGL and visibility 3,000 meters.

### 4.3 Alternate Airport Weather Minima
The alternate airport weather at the ETA must be at or above:
- For precision approach: Ceiling at least **200 feet above** the published Decision Height, and visibility at least **1,000 meters above** the published approach minimum RVR.
- For non-precision approach: Ceiling at least **400 feet above** the published Minimum Descent Altitude, and visibility at least **1,500 meters above** the published minimum.
- Alternate must have at least one operational approach aid.

### 4.4 Weather-Related Dispatch Restrictions
- **Volcanic Ash**: No dispatch into airspace with reported or forecast volcanic ash unless approved by the Chief Dispatcher with specific routing to avoid contaminated airspace.
- **Severe Turbulence**: Avoid areas of forecast severe or extreme turbulence. If unavoidable, brief crew and issue passenger safety advisory.
- **Thunderstorms**: If destination has active thunderstorm activity at ETA, ensure alternate is available with clear weather. Consider fuel for holding.
- **Icing**: Aircraft must have operative anti-icing/de-icing systems. For dispatch into known icing, all ice protection systems must be functional (no MEL deferral of ice protection components).
- **Sandstorm/Dust Storm**: Treat as reduced visibility. Apply published minima. Consider engine ingestion risk — may require post-flight engine inspection.

## SOP-005: NOTAM Review Procedures

### 5.1 NOTAM Sources
The dispatch team shall obtain NOTAMs from:
- AFTN (Aeronautical Fixed Telecommunication Network) via the NOTAM office.
- Operator's automated NOTAM retrieval system.
- Jeppesen or Lido electronic NOTAMs.
- ICAO NOTAM database (as backup/cross-reference).

### 5.2 NOTAM Review Process
1. **Retrieve**: Pull all active NOTAMs for departure, en-route, destination, and alternate aerodromes, plus all FIRs along the route.
2. **Filter**: Remove NOTAMs that are not applicable to the flight (e.g., NOTAMs for helicopter operations, military-specific NOTAMs, NOTAMs for equipment the aircraft does not carry).
3. **Categorize**: Sort NOTAMs into:
   - **Critical**: Runway closures, navigation aid outages affecting the planned approach, airspace closures along the route.
   - **Important**: Taxiway restrictions, apron changes, approach procedure changes, temporary obstacles.
   - **Informational**: Construction activity, crane operations, changed operating hours, local requirements.
4. **Assess Impact**: For each critical NOTAM, assess the operational impact:
   - Does it require a route change?
   - Does it affect the planned approach at destination?
   - Does it require a change of alternate airport?
   - Does it affect takeoff performance (e.g., displaced threshold, reduced runway length)?
5. **Brief**: Prepare a NOTAM summary for the crew briefing package. Highlight all critical and important NOTAMs.
6. **Sign Off**: Both the dispatcher and PIC shall acknowledge the NOTAM review with electronic or physical signature.

### 5.3 NOTAM Update Protocol
- NOTAMs shall be reviewed no earlier than **2 hours before departure**.
- A final NOTAM check shall be performed **30 minutes before departure**. If any new critical NOTAM has been issued, the crew and dispatcher shall be immediately notified.
- For long-haul flights, an en-route NOTAM update shall be sent via ACARS at waypoints or at least every **4 hours**.

## SOP-006: Aircraft Swap Procedures

### 6.1 When Aircraft Swap Is Required
- Unscheduled maintenance that cannot be completed before departure.
- MEL deferral count exceeds limits.
- AD compliance issue discovered.
- Aircraft damage (bird strike, ground damage, lightning strike) requiring inspection.
- Operational requirement (payload limitation, range limitation for the planned route).

### 6.2 Aircraft Swap Process
1. **Identification**: The dispatcher or maintenance control identifies the need for an aircraft swap.
2. **Substitute Aircraft Selection**: Operations Control identifies a substitute aircraft from:
   - Spare aircraft at the station (first priority).
   - Aircraft from a lower-priority flight that can be delayed or cancelled.
   - Aircraft from another station (ferry/reposition — last resort).
3. **Compatibility Check**: Before confirming the swap:
   - Verify the substitute aircraft type is approved for the route (runway length, navigation equipment, ETOPS capability).
   - Verify the assigned crew is type-rated for the substitute aircraft.
   - Verify the substitute aircraft has sufficient technical status (MEL items, AD compliance, C of A validity).
   - Verify passenger capacity matches or exceeds the booking load.
   - Verify cargo capacity meets requirements.
4. **Complete Re-Dispatch**: A full dispatch process must be restarted for the substitute aircraft:
   - New weight and balance calculation.
   - New performance calculations.
   - New fuel plan.
   - New MEL review.
   - New ATC flight plan (if aircraft type changed).
5. **Stakeholder Notification**:
   - ATC: New flight plan filed.
   - Ground handling: New gate, catering, and ground equipment requirements.
   - Passengers: Informed of delay and, if aircraft type changed, seat reassignment.
   - Crew: Briefed on the substitute aircraft status.
6. **Documentation**: Record the swap in the dispatch system with the original and substitute aircraft registrations, reason for swap, and delay incurred.

### 6.3 Maximum Acceptable Delay for Aircraft Swap
- **Domestic flights**: Up to 2 hours before considering cancellation.
- **International flights**: Up to 3 hours before considering cancellation.
- Escalate to Level 3 (Duty Manager — IOCC) if delay exceeds these thresholds.

## SOP-007: Disruption Handling Protocols

### 7.1 Disruption Categories

| Category | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Green** (Normal) | Minor delays within tolerance | Delay < 30 minutes, minor MEL deferral |
| **Amber** (Moderate) | Significant disruption affecting multiple flights | Delay 30–120 minutes, aircraft swap required, weather deterioration at destination |
| **Red** (Major) | Severe disruption with widespread impact | Airport closure, fleet grounding (AD), severe weather system, crew shortage affecting >5 flights |
| **Black** (Crisis) | Catastrophic event | Accident/incident, security threat, volcanic eruption, pandemic |

### 7.2 Response Protocols by Category

#### Green — Normal Disruption
- **Managed by**: Duty Dispatcher
- **Actions**: Standard delay management, update ETD, notify passengers via DCS/app.
- **Escalation**: Only if delay exceeds 30 minutes or passenger impact is significant.

#### Amber — Moderate Disruption
- **Managed by**: Senior Dispatcher + Duty Manager IOCC
- **Actions**:
  1. Activate disruption management workstream.
  2. Assess impact on downstream flights (connections, crew rotations, aircraft rotations).
  3. Implement recovery plan:
     - Crew swap if crew duty time is a factor.
     - Aircraft swap if maintenance is a factor.
     - Route the flight via alternate if weather is a factor.
  4. Communicate proactively with affected passengers (SMS, app notification, airport announcements).
  5. Arrange passenger rebooking for missed connections.
  6. Document all decisions in the disruption log.

#### Red — Major Disruption
- **Managed by**: IOCC Crisis Team (Chief Dispatcher + Crew Control Manager + Maintenance Control Manager + Passenger Services Manager)
- **Actions**:
  1. Activate IOCC crisis room.
  2. Convene crisis conference call within 15 minutes.
  3. Assess fleet-wide impact and develop a recovery plan.
  4. Prioritize flights based on: passenger numbers, connection criticality, international commitments, regulatory requirements.
  5. Implement mass rebooking and accommodation arrangements for stranded passengers.
  6. Coordinate with airport authorities and ATC for slot management.
  7. Issue operational directive to all stations.
  8. Media/PR team briefed for potential public communications.
  9. Regular status updates every 30 minutes until recovery.

#### Black — Crisis
- **Managed by**: Emergency Response Committee chaired by the Accountable Manager (CEO)
- **Actions**: Per the Emergency Response Plan (ERP). All normal operations may be suspended pending assessment. Coordination with DGCA, AAIB, airport authorities, and emergency services as required.

### 7.3 Recovery Principles
1. **Safety First**: No recovery action shall compromise safety. Do not pressure crews to accept marginal conditions to recover from disruption.
2. **Passenger Impact**: Minimize the total number of affected passengers across the network, not just on the disrupted flight.
3. **Cascading Prevention**: Act early to prevent disruption from cascading to downstream flights.
4. **Communication**: Proactive, timely, and honest communication with passengers, crew, and authorities.
5. **Documentation**: Every disruption event and the response actions shall be documented in the OCC disruption log for post-event review.
6. **Post-Disruption Review**: Every Red and Black disruption shall trigger a formal review within 7 days, with lessons learned documented and process improvements implemented.
